Neuroprosthetics In Human Paralysis 

Focal Publication | November, 2022

 

 

 

Introduction:

Neuroprothetics encompass any output neural interface that can convert the brain’s intentions to external actions. These series of devices can substitute a motor, sensory, or cognitive modality or degeneration that might have resulted from damage from an injury or disease. Related to both neuroscience and biomedical engineering, neuroprosthetics, such as cochlear implants and visual prosthetics, are just beginning to emerge as viable interventions in the field of brain injury. The objective of neuroprosthetics is to rehabilitate individuals handicapped by injury through promoting independence and enhancing their quality of life.

Neural Prostheses Function:

Using a brain-computer interface (BCI) that bridges the brain and prosthetic devices, certain thoughts, like moving or flexing a muscle, create an electrical activity which in turn activates nerve cells and triggers brainwaves. Neuroprosthetics differs from traditional prosthetics because it is more closely aligned to how the human body functions. The brain sends electrical impulses that ultimately cause that limb to perform the desired task when a body part is moved. To translate signals, a BCI relies on either a chip implanted in the individual’s brain or electrodes. Electrodes on the scalp work sufficiently for simple control such as bending and straightening an arm, but electrodes implanted in a brain, or intraparenchymal electrodes, offer greater fine motor control like the ability to control arms.

Individuals Affected by Paralysis:

Currently, the most common form of neuroprosthetics are the cochlear implants that substitute the functions of the eardrum and stapes while simulating the frequency produced by the cochlea. However, these devices intend to extend to those affected by paralysis and disabilities. Paralysis resulting from neurological injury or disease presents a significant burden on patients, their families, and caregivers. Neuroprostheses offer the promise of restoring movement to paralyzed patients by bypassing damaged regions of the nervous system, ultimately allowing patients the ability to live with greater independence and improving their quality of life. Recent advancements in neuroprosthetics have provided a groundbreaking neurotechnological solution for restoring function in paralysis. 

Process for the Paralyzed:

Neural prostheses relied on the combination of an implanted electrode array in the motor cortex and functional electrical stimulation (FES) to muscles in the paralyzed wrist. Neuroprosthetics that combine BCI with FES can restore voluntary control of a patients’ own paralyzed limbs. Human studies have demonstrated an “all-or-none” control, like hand-open and hand-closed, where there is no middle ground. In order to make up for unnatural motion from the prosthetics, a BCI-FES system enables smooth control of limb movements through a continuum of states thus allowing the functionality of BCI-FES neuroprosthetics to manipulate objects of different sizes without dropping or crushing them. These innovations provide the groundwork for enhanced and more natural motor control of paralyzed limbs

Past Studies and Successes:

First, primates have provided ample evidence that neutrons in the motor cortex carry information related to muscle activation and force exertion that predict electromyography activity and dictate electrical stimulation in the primate’s arm muscle. Non-human primates regained control of their legs ­just six days following a spinal injury thanks to a neuroprosthetic interface that acted like a “wireless bridge” between the brain and spine, skipping the injury and restoring functionality to their limbs. Normally, lesions in the spinal cord partly or completely inhibit signals from the motor cortex from reaching the neurons that activate leg muscles, ultimately leading to paralysis. But, regardless of the accident, the motor cortex can still produce spiking activity through walking, and the neural networks activating muscles in a paralyzed leg are still intact and can still properly generate leg movements and contractions. Spontaneously regaining complete mobility after about three months, these primates could properly walk once the brain-spine interface was activated. Similar to primate studies, functional imaging experiments in humans have shown that increasing force production is associated with increases in blood oxygen level-dependent signals in motor regions. Clearly the developments in neuroprosthetics led to studies on humans that have been affected by spinal cord injuries that have affected the brain. 

Restoring Muscle Movement:

Using the foundation that neuroprosthetics do in fact benefit the individual, a particular study demonstrates two important advances in restoring natural muscle contractions in paralyzed people. First, information related to graded muscle contraction intent is encoded in the motor cortex and can be decoded from intracortical recorded data. Second, tetraplegic humans can use this information and a BCI-controlled, non-invasive FES to regain control of graded muscle contraction in his paralyzed limb. The study observed distinct neural activity as the participant was asked to imagine exerting force to control a needle on a computer screen and match it to graded cue angles on a virtual protractor. The participant was then able to control the amplitude of FES to his own muscles to generate graded wrist flexion force against a resistance using his own thoughts. In addition to providing insights into the relationship between cortical neuronal activity and muscle contraction during voluntary hand movements, results also demonstrate an important advancement of neuroprosthetic technology that can help people suffering from paralysis regain the voluntary and smooth graded control of muscle activation that is necessary for manipulation of objects.

The study provides the first demonstration of continuous control of muscle activation in people affected by paralysis. Grasping delicate objects with varying weights with the correct amount of force are crucial for performing fine motor tasks that have now been uncovered by this study. On-going studies persist to enhance the natural behavior of the neuroprosthetic to mimic a human arm. Restoring grade muscle contractions will allow paralyzed individuals to gain functional independence, and neuroprosthetic technology is becoming a leading method to help those affected by stroke, immediate brain trauma, motor neuron diseases, and degenerative diseases like Parkinson’s. The potential for neuroprosthetics is unlimited and will continue to become more relevant. Neuroprosthetics is a growing field that has the potential to re-engineer the medical world. 

Obstacles to Development:

Cost appears to be a looming obstacle in potential future revitalizing devices such as retinal implants and neuroprosthetic limbs, as they are roughly three to five times more costly than cochlear implants. Regulatory hurdles for approval and reimbursement also appear to be a hurdle in the process because companies are working towards global commercialization.
